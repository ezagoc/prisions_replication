\section{Motivation}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}

\small

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
    \item Institutional actors respond to constraints.
    \pause
    \item In criminal justice, these constraints may be:
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
        \item \alert{Fiscal and private} — incarceration is costly {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Ouss-Incentives, Galinato-2020, Mukherjee-Private}}, 
        \item \alert{Electoral} — decisions may respond to political incentives {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Gordon-Judges-Election, Berdejo-PoliticalCycles}}, 
        \item \alert{Administrative} — higher caseloads reduce time to process cases {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Yang-Resource-AEJ, JudgeCaseloads-JPE-Shumway}}, % more incentives to avoid trial and look for plea deals
        \item \alert{Capacity-related} — limited prison space restricts the use of incarceration {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Schnepel}}. 
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}

\small

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
    
    \item If behavior changes when such constraints bind, then relaxing them may also change judicial outcomes.
    \pause
    \item \textbf{Research question:} This paper studies whether exogenous increases in prison capacity affect judicial and policing behavior.
    \pause

    \item Causal evidence on this question is scarce for two main reasons:
    \begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
        \item \textbf{Criminal justice outcomes are shaped by many actors:} judges, prosecutors, police, and prison authorities {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Ater-Police-Org-JPE}.}
        \pause
        \item \textbf{Increases in prison capacity are endogenous} to prior incarceration levels and local demand for prison space.
    \end{enumerate}

    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Strategy}
\small

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
    \item I exploit the staggered construction of \textbf{federal} prisons across Mexico as plausibly exogenous shocks to \textbf{local} prison capacity.
    \pause
    \item Where federal jurisdiction covers offenses prosecuted under federal law, while local state jurisdiction covers offenses prosecuted under state law and handled by state-level courts.
    \pause
    \item The identification strategy relies on three institutional features:
    \begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
        \item Judges must assign inmates to the nearest available facility.
        \pause
        \item New federal prisons were built to relocate federal inmates, not to address local capacity constraints.
        \pause
        \item State prisons also house federal prisoners.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{1) Inmate Assignment}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.7em}
    \item Judges are required to assign inmates to the \textbf{nearest available facility} relative to their municipality of residence.
    
    \item \textbf{Legal basis:} \textit{“Sentenced individuals may serve their sentence in the prison facilities closest to their place of residence.”}\\
    \hfill (Ley Nacional de Ejecución Penal, Article 49)
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{2) Federal prison expansion (2000 - 2012)}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}

\item To address federal under-capacity issues (2 federal prisons existed), the federal government launched a \textbf{federal} prison expansion program.
\pause
 \item Staggered, 8 public (2000-2012) and 10 private (2012-2018)
 \pause 
 
 \item Hand in hand with a federal change in policing by PAN (major crackdown on cartels \citep{DellMexicanDrug, AnnualReviewPoliSci}).

 \pause 
 \item Confirmed by the actual data: prosecutions increased at the federal level (2006–2012). 
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Preview of findings}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.7em}

    \item \textbf{Validation:} First I show that the \alert{construction of federal prisons} significantly \alert{decreased the overcrowding of state prisons}.
    \pause
    \item \textbf{Main result}: Federal prison construction leads to \alert{increases in pretrial processing} and in the \alert{number of individuals sentenced}.
    \pause
    \item Municipalities closer to new federal prisons experience:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Higher \alert{local (state) incarceration rates}
        \item A greater likelihood of \alert{pretrial detention}
        \item \alert{No change} in monetary fines
    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    \item \textbf{Mechanisms:} Results are consistent with changes in \textbf{judicial behavior driven by capacity constraints}, rather than solely by increased caseloads or resource constraints, although these channels cannot be fully ruled out.
    
    
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Theory}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Theory and Context}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.7em}
    
    \item Existing work highlights three channels through which criminal justice outcomes may respond to institutional constraints:
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.5em}
        \item \textbf{Salience}: incarceration becomes more likely when prison is a more salient punishment {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Galinato-2020}.}
        \item \textbf{Fiscal / capacity}: incarceration falls when prison is more costly or space is limited {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Ouss-Incentives}.}
        \item \textbf{Time}: heavier caseloads can alter case processing and sentencing decisions {\footnotesize\color{gray}\citep{Yang-Resource-AEJ}.}
    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    
    \item In this context, the most relevant channel is \textbf{capacity}: nearby federal prison construction relaxes local prison constraints.
    \pause
    
    \item By contrast, the electoral and local fiscal incentives emphasized in prior U.S. work are less likely to operate in the same way in the Mexican case.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Interpretation and Caveats}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.7em}
    
    \item \textbf{Interpretation:} The findings --- more pretrial processing, more pretrial detention, more prison sentences, and no change in monetary fines --- suggest that relaxing capacity increases the use of incarceration.
    \pause
    
    \item This pattern is more consistent with a capacity-based explanation than with a purely administrative response.
    \pause
    
    \item \textbf{Caveat:} Capacity expansions may also increase policing and case inflows, so part of the effect may reflect higher caseload pressure rather than judicial behavior alone.
    \pause
    
    \item I therefore interpret the results as evidence that relaxing capacity constraints changes criminal justice outcomes, while the precise margin remains harder to fully disentangle.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Data}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
    \item Two administrative datasets covering all formally processed individuals in Mexico (local level):
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
        \item \textbf{Pre-trial dataset}: early judicial decisions (detention vs.\ release) % why would 
        \item \textbf{Criminal courts dataset}: final outcomes (sentences, fines, acquittals)
    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    \item Both include rich individual-level information that lets me observe the \alert{municipality of residence}.
    \item Records aggregated to \alert{municipality $\times$ bimonth} level, matched with geolocated data on \alert{local prison capacity and population}.
    
    
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Empirical Strategy}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Empirical Strategy}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.65em}
\item I estimate a generalized difference-in-difference design with staggered adoption \citep{Callaway}.

    \begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq1}
    Y_{mt} = \phi + \beta TreatPost_{tm} + \gamma_t + \tau_m + \epsilon_{mt},
\end{equation}
\small
\item $TreatPost_{tm}$: time-varying dummy variable that equals one if, at time $t$, the closest federal prison to a municipality is within 300 kilometers (KM). (Robust to other definitions) % Second, the choice of the 300KM threshold was taken by a data-driven approach. This distance captures both the median (207 km) and mean (212 km) minimum distances from each municipality to a federal prison, providing adequate variation for the new prison’s construction to affect relevant municipalities
\pause
\item Outcomes are total counts of the variables, and I use the log transformation to handle the presence of outliers (robust to other transformations)
\pause
 \item \textbf{Interpretation (Generalized DiD):} The estimate compares how sentencing behavior changed in municipalities close to where a new prison opened — before versus after the opening — against municipalities where no prison opened over the same period.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Conclusion}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Conclusion}
\small

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.8em}
    \item I provide evidence that exogenous increases in prison capacity affect criminal justice outcomes.
    
    \item Federal prison construction relieves overcrowding in local state prisons and increases pretrial processing, pretrial detention, and prison sentences.
    
    \item More broadly, incarceration rates may be shaped not only by underlying crime and legal criteria, but also by institutional capacity.
    
    \item In that sense, prison construction may do more than absorb existing prisoners: it may change the behavior of the criminal justice system itself.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
